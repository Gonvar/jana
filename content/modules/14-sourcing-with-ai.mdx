Module 9 gave you one sourcing recipe. This module turns that idea into a fuller practice.

Sourcing is one of the best places to use AI because so much of the work sits around the decision rather than inside it: turning a vague brief into criteria, widening the option set, organising comparisons, and drafting cleaner supplier communication. The danger is obvious too. A model can sound confident about products, availability, lead times, or pricing that it does not actually know.

Used well, AI becomes a fast sourcing assistant. Used badly, it becomes a polite hallucination machine.

<Analogy title="The junior sourcing assistant">
Think of AI as a very fast junior sourcing assistant. It can organise the brief, expand the search, draft the first comparison table, and prepare supplier emails. It should not be the person who promises the client that something is in stock, on budget, or arriving in six weeks.
</Analogy>

## Step 1: Turn taste into criteria

Most sourcing problems start too vaguely. The brief says warm, quiet, natural, elevated, but not ostentatious. That may be enough for you to feel the direction, but it is not yet enough to source well.

AI is useful here because it can turn aesthetic language into a checklist:

- What is essential
- What is flexible
- What budget tier this implies
- What lead-time pressure exists
- What must be verified before specifying anything

This first move matters more than people think. A bad sourcing search is usually not a search problem. It is a criteria problem.

## Step 2: Expand the options without drifting off-brief

Once the criteria are clear, use AI to widen the field without losing the brief.

Ask for:

- Alternative materials that preserve the same feeling
- Lower-cost and higher-cost versions of the same direction
- Adjacent styles that still make sense for the scheme
- Categories you may have forgotten to specify
- Risks attached to each route, such as maintenance, fragility, or visual heaviness

This is where AI is particularly helpful for unfamiliar categories. It has absorbed a large amount of language around materials, periods, finishes, and product types. That makes it good at offering plausible directions you can then test against reality.

## Step 3: Compare like with like

The fastest way to make sourcing easier is to stop comparing options in different formats.

Ask AI to turn every option into the same structure:

- Product or category name
- Dimensions
- Material and finish
- Price basis
- Lead-time note
- Installation or maintenance risk
- Reason it fits the brief

Once everything is normalised, your judgment becomes clearer. You are no longer deciding between a beautiful paragraph, a supplier PDF, and a half-remembered note from a conversation. You are deciding between comparable options.

## Step 4: Use AI for supplier communication

Supplier communication is another strong use case because consistency matters.

AI can help you:

- Draft a clean RFQ
- Write a follow-up that asks only for the missing information
- Turn your notes into a supplier-ready specification summary
- Rewrite a vague internal note as a precise supplier question

The benefit is not that the writing becomes prettier. It is that every supplier gets the same core ask, which makes replies easier to compare.

## The verification loop

Before anything reaches the client as a recommendation, verify the parts that AI cannot know:

- Actual availability
- Actual lead time
- Actual price and trade terms
- Actual dimensions and technical suitability
- Actual finish, sample quality, and compliance

This is the professional line. AI may structure the field. The final promise still belongs to you.

If the output is useful, do not stop there. Replace generic language with the actual names, prices, and facts you have gathered. The goal is not to outsource sourcing. It is to remove the messy first pass so your real expertise lands where it matters.

<KeyIdea>
AI is excellent at helping you run a better sourcing process. It is not a live supplier database, and it is not the person who should make unverified promises on your behalf.
</KeyIdea>
