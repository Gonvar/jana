Once AI enters paid client work, the question changes. Up to this point AI may have felt like a speed tool. Here it also becomes a rights, disclosure, and trust tool.

The important question is not simply, can I generate this? It is: who authored it, what would I need to say about it, and what record would I want if a client, platform, or registry asked questions later?

This module is a practical working map for designers in Spain and the EU. It is not a substitute for legal advice on a dispute, a campaign, or a trademark problem. But it is enough to keep you out of a large amount of avoidable trouble.

## The legal baseline in Spain

Spanish copyright law still starts from a human author. The Ley de Propiedad Intelectual says rights belong to the author by the mere fact of creation, and it speaks in terms of the author as a natural person. The law does not yet contain a neat AI clause that resolves every mixed human-and-machine workflow.

That means something practical. The more the expressive work is clearly yours — the selection, composition, redrawing, editing, material judgment, annotation, and final shaping — the stronger your authorship story is. The more a deliverable is simply machine output with very little human intervention, the weaker that story becomes.

<Analogy title="The tracing paper test">
Imagine placing tracing paper over a rough underlay and redrawing the composition, proportions, material decisions, and annotations yourself. The professional and legal weight sits in what you actually contribute, not in the underlay alone. AI output is similar: it may be useful as a starting surface, but the authorship question turns on the human work that follows.
</Analogy>

## What the EU AI Act actually changes

This is where a lot of people become either too relaxed or too dramatic.

The EU AI Act entered into force on 1 August 2024. The transparency rules most relevant to designers apply from **2 August 2026**. Those rules are narrower than the internet often suggests.

For this course, the practical points are:

- Providers of generative AI systems must make synthetic outputs detectable in specified ways
- Deployers must disclose certain deepfake-style image, audio, or video content
- There are also rules for certain AI-generated text published to inform the public on matters of public interest

That is not the same as a general EU rule saying every AI-assisted moodboard, draft, render, or client email must be labeled.

For most ordinary studio work, the AI Act does **not** create a blanket obligation to announce every use of AI. But if you are using realistic synthetic people, materially manipulated media, or public-facing informational content, the disclosure question becomes much more concrete.

## Disclosure as professional practice

In design work, the more useful question is often not, am I legally forced to disclose this? It is, would a reasonable client expect to know?

Disclosure is usually the right move when:

- AI made a material contribution to the deliverable, not just a minor editing pass
- The provenance or ownership story is not obvious from the final work
- The work could raise rights-clearance questions later
- You are using generated people, voices, or other content that could be mistaken for something real
- The client has confidentiality, security, or procurement rules about what tools may be used

By contrast, if AI helped you rewrite an internal recap or tighten a first draft of your own email, that is usually a workflow choice rather than a major disclosure event.

The point is not ceremony. The point is trust. Clients rarely object to tools. They object to surprises.

## The record that protects you

When AI is part of the workflow, keep a simple working record:

- Which tool was used, and for what stage
- What source material went in
- What the AI produced
- What you changed, selected, rejected, or rebuilt yourself
- What was verified manually before anything went to the client

This sounds administrative, and it is. It is also what allows you to answer awkward questions later without reconstructing the whole story from memory.

If client material includes personal data, confidential information, or commercially sensitive details, check that the tool and the agreement allow that use before you paste anything in.

## Three situations where risk rises quickly

**1. Brand marks and distinctive graphics.** AI is good at producing plausible visual forms. That does not mean the result is safe to use as a logo or brand asset. Similarity, originality, and clearance are different questions.

**2. Synthetic people or spokesperson-style imagery.** If you create realistic generated or manipulated content that could be mistaken for authentic media, the disclosure question is much sharper under the AI Act.

**3. Rights-sensitive client work.** If the client cares about ownership, exclusivity, provenance, or confidentiality, you need a clearer paper trail than "we used AI somewhere in the process."

## A simple working rule

Treat AI as an assistant, not a ghost author.

Let it help you explore, draft, vary, and organise. But make sure the human contribution is real, visible, and documentable. That is the safest place to stand both legally and professionally.
