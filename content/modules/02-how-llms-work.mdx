In the previous module, we established what AI is: a pattern-matching system trained on an enormous amount of human writing. Now let's go one level deeper — not into mathematics, but into the actual mechanic of how it generates a response. This will explain a lot of things that otherwise seem mysterious, including why AI sometimes says something confidently wrong.

## One word at a time

When you send a message to Claude or ChatGPT, the model does not think about your question, retrieve an answer from a database, and send it back. It does something far stranger and more interesting.

It reads your message, and then — one small unit of language at a time — it predicts what should come next.

<Analogy title="The prediction machine">
Imagine you are mid-sentence and you pause. Someone who knows language well can often guess your next word — not because they know what you mean to say, but because they have heard so many sentences that certain words follow certain other words with predictable frequency. "The client preferred a more—" invites "neutral" or "contemporary" far more than "purple" or "catastrophic". A language model does this, but trained on billions of examples, running at high speed, making thousands of these small predictions in sequence to build a full response.
</Analogy>

Each prediction influences the next. The model is not choosing from a pre-written list of possible answers. It is composing your response word by word, each word shaped by everything that came before it.

## The hidden thinking that happens first

With the most capable models, there is a step before the visible response that is worth knowing about.

When you ask a complex question, the model does not go straight to answering. It first generates a private chain of reasoning — thinking through the problem step by step, testing approaches, catching its own mistakes — before producing the response you see. These are sometimes called *thinking tokens*: the model's internal working-out, invisible to you but shaping everything that follows.

This is why asking a good model to reason through a decision — *"think through the pros and cons before recommending"* — often produces a noticeably better answer than asking it to respond immediately. You are giving the model permission to use the process it is best at.

## How the model was trained

The model did not arrive knowing any of this. It was trained — a process that took months of computation and hundreds of billions of examples.

The training process is conceptually simple: show the model a piece of text, hide the last word, ask it to predict what the word should be, compare its guess to the real word, and adjust the model's internal settings slightly so it does a little better next time. Repeat this billions of times across an enormous amount of text.

<Glossary term="training" definition="The process of exposing an AI model to vast amounts of data and adjusting its internal parameters until it learns to predict patterns accurately." />

After enough repetitions, the model develops something that functions like an understanding of language — not because anyone taught it grammar or vocabulary directly, but because patterns in the data made those concepts necessary to predict well. Grammar is not a rule the model was given; it is a structure the model discovered because following it makes better predictions.

## Why it sometimes makes things up

This is the part that surprises most people, and once you understand it, it changes how you work with AI forever.

The model was trained to produce *plausible* next words — not *true* ones. Plausibility and truth overlap a great deal, but they are not the same thing.

<Analogy title="The very confident intern">
Imagine a very bright, very eager intern on their first week. They have read everything they could find about your industry, they speak fluently about it, and they never say "I don't know" — because they have been trained (in a social sense) to appear competent. When they don't know the answer, they produce a confident-sounding answer assembled from the relevant things they have read. It is often close. Sometimes it is wrong in ways that are hard to spot, because it sounds exactly like how a correct answer would sound.
</Analogy>

When an AI model tells you a specific product has a certain fire rating, or that a building regulation requires a specific clearance, it is producing text that *looks like* what that answer would look like. It may be right. It may be subtly wrong. It has no access to a database of facts — only to the patterns of language it was trained on.

<Glossary term="hallucination" definition="When an AI model generates something that sounds accurate but is factually incorrect — not a deliberate lie, but a confident wrong prediction." />

This is called hallucination. The good news is that top models have improved dramatically: in 2024, roughly 8–12% of high-stakes queries contained factual errors. By 2026, the best models produce factual hallucinations in less than 1% of responses — a reduction of over 90%. Better models now explicitly say *"I don't know"* when uncertain rather than guessing.

However, two areas remain higher risk: anything involving code or software (where APIs and libraries change faster than training data can keep up), and anything requiring real-time information the model simply cannot have. For these, verify regardless of how confident the output sounds.

**The practical rule:** trust the model with language tasks — tone, structure, phrasing, synthesis. Verify it for facts, measurements, regulations, product specifications, and anything where being wrong has real consequences.

## Memory: the picture is changing

For most of the history of these tools, each conversation started fresh. The model had no memory of previous sessions — every time you opened a new chat, you were starting from zero.

This is still true of the underlying model itself. But in 2026, most major tools — Claude, ChatGPT, Gemini — offer persistent memory features that store key facts between sessions and automatically surface them when relevant. If you tell the tool your client prefers contemporary Nordic interiors, it can remember that next time.

This is worth knowing because the rules around what to re-state at the start of a conversation are shifting. Within a conversation, you still need to front-load important context. But across conversations, a tool with memory enabled may already know things you would otherwise have to repeat.

## What emerges from all of this

What is remarkable is that something so mechanistic — predict the next word, correct, repeat — produces output that can feel genuinely thoughtful. A well-prompted model can write with clarity, synthesise complex information, find the right tone for a difficult message, and make connections across ideas. Not because it is thinking, but because good thinking, like good language, leaves patterns that can be learned.

This is worth sitting with. It is not lesser for being mechanical. It is impressive precisely because the mechanism is so much simpler than you would expect for the result it produces.
