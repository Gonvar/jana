Client communication is one of the best places to use AI in design work because the task is repetitive, but the relationship still matters.

You are not asking AI to replace your judgment. You are asking it to help you move faster through the language work around the judgment: the recap, the follow-up, the tidy summary, the tone shift, the version for a different stakeholder.

Used well, AI saves time here without making the work feel robotic. Used badly, it turns into the exact thing clients dislike: a message that is technically clear but emotionally flat, generic, and a little off.

## Where AI actually helps

The best use cases are the ones that are high-frequency and low-creativity:

- Turning rough meeting notes into a clean recap with action items
- Compressing long email threads into what matters, what is still open, and who owes what
- Drafting first-pass client updates from bullet points
- Rewriting a message to sound warmer, firmer, or more concise without changing the facts
- Creating two or three versions of the same update for different audiences, such as client, contractor, and internal team
- Pulling a polite subject line or opening sentence out of a clumsy draft

<Analogy title="The studio coordinator">
Think of AI as the best studio coordinator on the team. It can sort the notes, draft the follow-up, and keep the thread moving. It should not decide the message, own the relationship, or make promises on your behalf.
</Analogy>

The real time saving comes from removing the blank page and the admin friction. You still make the call on tone, content, and escalation.

## How to keep it human

The trick is to give AI enough context to sound like a person who actually knows the project.

1. Start from your real notes, not from a vague request for "a professional email."
2. Tell it who the client is in relational terms. Warm, direct, formal, detail-oriented, anxious, decisive.
3. Ask for a tone that sounds like you, not like an assistant trying to impress.
4. Keep the facts fixed. AI can rephrase them, but it should not invent timelines, approvals, or decisions.
5. If the notes include confidential budgets, personal data, or sensitive project details, make sure the tool is appropriate before you paste them in.
6. Ask for more than one version when the message is sensitive. The best wording is often a blend of two drafts.

If the message changes expectations, acknowledges a mistake, or touches budget and scope, AI should help you write it, but you should still own the final wording.

## What not to delegate

There are a few lines AI should not cross in client communication:

- Do not let it invent project facts, dates, or commitments.
- Do not let it soften a hard message so much that it becomes unclear.
- Do not let it write apologies that sound vague or performative.
- Do not let it hide a scope change inside pretty language.
- Do not send a draft without checking whether it matches the actual project status.

The fastest way to lose trust is to send something polished that is slightly wrong.

## Three realistic scenarios

### 1. After a client review

You have just finished a site visit. The client liked the direction, but asked for two changes and raised one open question about joinery costs. AI is useful here for turning your quick notes into a recap that says what was agreed, what still needs confirmation, and what happens next.

This saves time because you do not have to rebuild the same message from scratch every time. It also helps avoid the common problem of a vague recap that leaves everyone with a different memory of the meeting.

### 2. A scope or budget shift

The client wants to keep the idea but the approved budget no longer supports the specification. This is exactly the kind of message where AI helps with phrasing but not with judgment.

Use it to draft a clear, calm note that names the issue, explains the implication, and opens the door to a constructive conversation. Do not ask it to "make it sound nicer" if that means making it less honest.

### 3. A long thread with too many people in it

The client, contractor, and fabricator have all replied to the same chain, and the thread has turned into a small maze. AI can summarize the thread into: what each person said, what is agreed, what is disputed, and what needs a decision.

That summary becomes the basis for a short, clean response that feels human because it is specific. It references the actual issue instead of sounding like a generic project update.

If the first pass sounds too stiff, ask for a second version in your own voice and then merge the parts that feel most natural. AI is usually better as an editor than as the first and final author.

<KeyIdea>
AI saves the most time in client communication when it handles the repetitive language work and you handle the relationship, the facts, and the judgment. The goal is faster communication that still sounds like a real designer talking to a real client.
</KeyIdea>
